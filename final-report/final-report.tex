\begin{abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

Efficient route planning on long-distance road networks should minimize not only travel
distance or time but also the total cost of fuel purchased along the trip.
That objective becomes non-trivial when fuel prices vary across stations and drivers are free
to choose both where to stop and how much fuel to buy.
This report studies the fuel-aware route optimization problem on Thailand road networks,
where the task is to jointly determine a feasible route and a refueling policy that minimizes
monetary fuel expenditure.

The transportation network is modelled as a weighted directed graph whose nodes represent
fuel stations and whose edges represent feasible travel segments.
Six algorithmic strategies are implemented and evaluated: State-Expanded Dijkstra, Greedy,
Dynamic Programming, Standard A*, Refuel A* (RF-A*), and the proposed Partial-Fill A*
(PF-A*).
PF-A* strengthens RF-A* with a fuel-level-aware heuristic that incorporates both the current
fuel state and the cheapest reachable station.
Its admissibility and consistency are formally proved.

The report consolidates the full problem formulation, the algorithmic framework, the data
model, and the current empirical evaluation pipeline for both synthetic benchmarks and real
Thai routes.
The available synthetic results already show that PF-A* matches the best observed costs of
the exact baselines while substantially reducing runtime and node expansions in the tested
configurations.
The final-report structure created here is also prepared to absorb the full Experiment~6
real-route evaluation and the final high-run synthetic results without requiring a redesign of
the document.
\end{abstract}
